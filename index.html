Social Determinants of Health¶

Analysis by Tamar Kellner

The conditions under which someone grows up and lives under greatly impact their quality of life, especially when it comes to health. According to the CDC, "Social determinants of health (SDOH) are conditions in the places where people live, learn, work, and play that affect a wide range of health risks and outcomes." While this is recognizable, it can be hard to identify which determinants to focus on improving. While all are important, there are not endless resources available to help improve the health of the population. This project aims to identify how impactful different demographic features can be on health outcomes.

To learn more about social determinants of health and goals surrounding this issue, visit the US Department of Health and Human Services' Healthy People 2030 initiative: https://health.gov/healthypeople/priority-areas/social-determinants-health

1. Data Collection¶

To analyze different social determinants of health in the United States, this tutorial will use data from the CDC's 2020 Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System Survey. The goal of this survey is to "Collect uniform state-specific data on health risk behaviors, chronic diseases and conditions, access to health care, and use of preventive health services related to the leading causes of death and disability in the United States." The dataset is available from the CDC in ASCII format, however it was also made available as a csv file on Kaggle.com. This tutorial will use the csv version, so we will need to load the dataset in using the Kaggle API.

To learn more about the survey, including how it was conducted and what data is available, navigate to: https://www.cdc.gov/brfss/annual_data/annual_2020.html

In order to load the dataset, you must first create an API access token on Kaggle. Do this with the following steps:

  1. Go to kaggle.com and log in (or create a free account if you don't already have one)
  2. Navigate to your profile in the upper righthand corner of the website
  3. Go to the account tab and scroll down to where it says API. Click on 'Create New API Token', and a kaggle.json file will automatically download to your computer

Next, use the following commands to install the Kaggle library, upload the kaggle.json file from the above step, and then download the specific csv file that will be used in this tutorial.

One important aspect of community health is chronic illness. According to the American Public Health Association, chronic diseases are the leading cause of death and disability in the U.S. and among the most costly and preventable.

For more information on the economics of chronic disease, read here: https://www.apha.org/~/media/files/pdf/factsheets/chronicdiseasefact_final.ashx

Eleven of the columns in our table are chronic illnesses, so we will use them in our analysis of population health:

  • Heart Attack
  • Angina or Coronary Heart Disease
  • Stroke
  • Asthma
  • Skin Cancer
  • Other Cancer
  • COPD
  • Arthritis
  • Depression
  • Kidney Disease
  • Diabetes

In order to utilize chronic health as a whole, add a column to the table that tracks how many chronic illnesses each respondent reports having.

In [10]:
chronic_diseases = ['Heart_Attack', 'Angina_HeartDisease', 'Stroke', 'Asthma', 'Skin_Cancer', 'Other_Cancer', 'COPD', 'Arthritis', 'Depression', 'Kidney_Disease', 'Diabetes']

# Given a row of the dataframe, sum up how many of the chronic diseases in the above list the respondent has been diagnoseed with.
def sum_cd(row):
    diseases = 0

    for cd in chronic_diseases:     
      # The Diabetes column has a 1 for yes and a 2 for yes but only diagnosed during pregnancy, so we will include both as cases. All other chronic diseases are only positive with a value of 1
      if cd == 'Diabetes':
        positives = [1,2]
      else:
        positives = [1]

      if row[cd] in positives:
        diseases += 1
    
    return diseases

survey_df['num_chronic_diseases'] = survey_df.apply(lambda row: sum_cd(row), axis=1)

The regression lines demonstrate that the number of chronic diseases someone has are a good indicator for health as a whole as they are both positive correlations.

As the number of chronic diseases increases, the distributions of days with poor mental and physical health go from being concentrated on the bottom to concentrated on the top and then to a more equal distribution. Many of the distributions are bi-modal, which could allude to an all-or-nothing mindset of respondents when talking about their health. Many either think of their health as pretty good most of the time or pretty bad most of the time, and it is only once we get to distributions for people with the highest number of chronic illnesses that we see more even distributions. This could possibly be because people with more diseases may actually be tracking their health more closely out of need. On the lower end of chronic diseases, there are some outliers that skew the distributions. In both of these violin plots we can look at the mean markers to see what was shown with the regression line on the scatter plots - as the number of chronic diseases someone has increases, both their physical and mental health deteriorate.

  • Healthcare Coverage

The P-value for every demographic feature used is below the alpha value of 0.05 except for that of the largest income bracket. Thus, the rest of the features are statistically significant for determining number of chronic diseases.

The features with the highest coefficients, and therefore the highest indicators of chronic disease, are:

  • Employment Status = 8.0 = Unable to Work
  • Age >= 65
  • Employment Status = 3.0 = Out of Work for 1 Year or More
  • Employment Status = 7.0 = Retired
  • Education = 3.0 = Attended College or Technical School
  • Race = 3.0 = Asian, Non-Hispanic

Some of these, especially employment status, may actually be a reverse correlation. Being unable to work is more likely a result of chronic disease, and therefor is not a factor that can be improved upon in order to decrease risk of chronic disease. The variables that can be focused on, however, are age, employment unrelated to ability to work, access to education, and racial inequities.

5. Access to Healthcare¶

We now know that education level is a significant factor in determining health outcomes. If people with lower levels of education are predisposed to have worse health, they are probably in greater need of healthcare more often. Let's explore what the distribution of healthcare coverage is like for respondents based on their education level.

Now that we are looking at each education level in a more normalized chart, it is clear that as level of education completed increases, healthcare coverage also increases. This means that those who have less education are both more likely to have more chronic diseases and less likely to have healthcare coverage. Lower education is often linked with unemployment and lower household income, which are also attributes that contribute to a higher risk for disease.

5. Conclusion¶

Based on the data from this survey, many demographic features are important determinants of health in the US population. Members of marginalized groups and the lower class are at the highest risk for chronic illness, and therefore lower physical and mental health overall. The groups that are most at risk for diseases are also the ones who have the least access to healthcare. This is a vicious cycle of not being able to get better due to limited access to healthcare and then getting more ill, and again needing healthcare that is unavailable.

In order to make a change to the US healthcare system, the focus must be on marginalized and lower class members of society. The basic human need of healthcare should be separated from classism and discrimination in order to best serve everyone in this country.
